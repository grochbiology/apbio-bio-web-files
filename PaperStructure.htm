Paper Structure

Introduction (1 paragraph)

The introduction should grab the reader's attention; give background information on your topic and end with the thesis statement. The introduction should be about one page.

Opener:                                     

Open up with a quote, interesting fact or a brief story.

Background Information:    

Define concepts; describe countries, people, places, and history associated with the topic. Pretend the reader knows nothing. Use your note cards and cite sources for background information.  Put the author’s last name and the page number of the book in parenthesis at the end of the sentence. (Author Pg.#).

Transitions:             

Use transitions throughout the paragraph to connect your sentences.

Thesis Statement: 

End the paragraph with your final thesis statement.

 

Body Paragraphs (3 to 4 paragraphs)

Each body paragraph should be an argument that supports or proves your thesis.  Write one paragraph for each argument. Your arguments should have concrete details and commentary that connects  to the thesis.  The paragraph should flow smoothly connected by transitions. Each body paragraph should be about one page long.

 Topic Sentence:    

Introduce your argument in the topic sentence.

 Concrete Detail #1:    

State the first example that supports or proves the argument.

Quote:                      

Use a quote from research.  Be sure to CITE THE SOURCE.  Put the author’s last name and the page number of the book in parenthesis at the end of the sentence. (Author #).

Commentary:         

Explain the concrete detail and the quote.  Then explain how the example proves the argument and the thesis.

Concrete Detail #2, Quote, Commentary       

Concrete Detail #3, Quote, Commentary    

Transitions:             

Use transitions throughout the paragraph to connect your sentences.

(The first example..., The second example..., Another example...)

 Conclusion Sentence:            

Restate your argument in the last sentence of the paragraph.

  

Conclusion (1 paragraph)

The conclusion should summarize your arguments and leave the reader feeling convinced you have proven your thesis.  A good conclusion shows you are organized and convincing. The conclusion should be one-half a page in length.

Topic Sentence:               

 Transition from your arguments

Restate Arguments:         

Clearly restate your arguments

Conclude:                           

Finish with strong concluding commentary and final thoughts

Option:                               

 You can start or end with a quote.  I prefer to end in my own words.
