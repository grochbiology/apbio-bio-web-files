Thermoregulation Lab

*(NOTE: do not participate in this lab activity if you have a known allergy to shellfish or iodine, please bring a parent note excusing you from participating in this lab. Complete the pre-lab questions in your journal and record your lab mates data to help you answer the post-lab questions.)

Objectives:

Demonstrate  sweat glands in skin.

Determine the sweat gland activity with a change of environmental temperature and body temperature.

Organizing data in tables.

Comparing effects of different experimental conditions.

Materials:

cotton balls, iodine, cotton swabs, 2 inch square copy paper, heat source, metric ruler

Background Questions:

1. What is homeostasis?

2. How is internal body temperature regulated in humans?

3. How does the internal body temperature of mammals and birds compare to that of reptiles and amphibians?

4. What are the sensory organs that communicate with the brain's temperature control center in the hypothalamus?

5. Write your own question in your journal.

Procedure:

1. Pick one area on your body where sweating occurs rapidly when you get nervous or too warm. (palm)

2. Swab the area with a cotton ball saturated with alcohol. Allow to dry completely.

3. Swab the dry area with the diluted iodine, allow to dry completely.

4. For each condition (no stimulus-control, heat (light bulb)  and exercise, allow (0 time, 5 minutes and 5 minutes) the appropriate time to pass before applying the paper to the area. Hold the paper still for 30 seconds, pressing firmly without moving the paper. Examine the paper for faint blue/black dots. Draw a 1 cm square and count the number of dots. Record in your data table.

5. Clean off the area with more alcohol to remove iodine.

Post Lab Questions:

1.  Where do you hypothesize that the greatest number of sweat glands are located: on the back of hand or forearm or on the forehead?

2. What do the dots on the paper represent?

3. Were there more dots on the paper before or after the heat exposure?

4. What does sweating accomplish for the body?

5. How is sweating an adaptation  that maintains homeostasis?

6. As you reacted to heat and exercise, what changes were observed in the sweat glands?

7. How do the sweat glands fit into the analogy of the body as a human thermostat?

8. Write a new question that could be explored as a new investigation.
