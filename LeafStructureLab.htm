Lab:  What are the structures of a leaf?

 

Background: The leaf of a plant is a specialized organ designed mainly for photosynthesis. With few exceptions, all other life depends on the functioning of the leaves of green plants. Various parts of the leaf structure perform different functions in helping the plant to live, to grow, and to produce food. This investigation will help you identify these structures. You will also see how leaves vary in size and shape. Leaves can be classified according to their type or form, the pattern of leaf margin, the arrangement of leaf veins, and the arrangement of leaves on the stem. The simplest types of leaves are found on mosses, which are called leaflets. Mosses are so small that they do not need a vascular system to distribute water and nutrients, osmosis and diffusion is enough. More complex plants include ferns, the conifers (gymnosperms), and flowering plants (angiosperms). Angiosperms are further divided into two categories; monocotyledons (monocots) with only one endosperm, and dicotyledons (dicots) with two endosperms.

 

Objectives:

Identify the main structures in a monocot, a dicot, and a conifer of a leaf

Classify leaves according to leaf type, venation, leaf margin, and leaf arrangement.

Identify the various tissues and structures observed in a leaf cross section.

Describe how some leaf structures function.

 

Materials:

Various leaves (monocot, dicot, gymnosperms (conifer), moss, ferns, hand lens.

 

Procedure:

  1. Review Chapter 22. Please note there are examples of leaf shapes, leaf types, leaf arrangement and leaf margins at the end of this document.
  2. Obtain an example of a dicot leaf. Note the color of its upper and lower surfaces in your journal.
  3. Most dicots have a blade, the broad part of the leaf; and a petiole, or the stalk that connects the leaf to the stem. Look for these parts on your leaf. Use a hand lens to observe the midrib, a large central vein that runs the length of the leaf.  Look for the smaller veins branching out from the midrib to all parts of the blade. Record your observations and draw and label your leaf in your journal.
  4. Identify the type of leaf type, leaf margin, leaf shapes, and leaf arrangement on several types of dicot leaves. “Simple” leaves are those with one whole blade. “Compound” leaves are made up of several divisions called leaflets attached to one petiole. Compound leaves can be further divided into two subgroups.  “Palmately compound” leaves have several leaflets that arise from the tip of the petiole. “Pinnately compound” have leaflets that run along opposite sides of an extension of the petiole. Record your observations and draw and label your leaf in your journal.
  5. Dicot leaves can be classified by venation, or vein pattern. In “parallel venation:”, several main veins run the length of the leaf. Most monocots hae parallel venation. A leaf with “net venation” contains a netlike pattern of veins spread throughout the leaf. Hold each leaf specimen up to the light, and identify the vein pattern. You may need to use a hand lens to see the veins. Record your observations in your journal.
  6. Dicot leaves can also be classified by the pattern of the leaf margin which include smooth, wavy, serrated (ouch), shallow lobed, deep lobed, bristle tipped. Record your observations and draw and label your leaf in your journal.
  7. Dicot leaves can also be classified by the type of arrangement of the leaves or leaflets on the stem (small branch). “Opposite arrangement” means the individual leaves are arranged opposite each other (do not get confused with a compound leaf with leaflets connected to one petiole). In “alternate arrangement” the leaves are attached at alternate positions on a stem. “Whorled arrangement” means several leaves arise together around one part of the stem. Record your observations and draw and label your leaf in your journal.
  8. Dicot leaves can also be classified by leaf shape. Go to the end of this document and identify the leaf shape of your samples. Record your observations for each leaf.
  9. Obtain a monocot leaf. Observe the shape of the leaf blade. Use a hand lends to examine the leaf veins. Notice that the lower edge of the blade surrounds the stem forming a sheath. Record your observations and draw and label your leaf in your journal.
  10. Obtain a conifer (gymnosperm) leaf such as a pine leaf and examine its shape. Pine leaves (needles) are found in clusters that are enclosed by a scaly sheath. Pine leaves grow in groups of 2 3, or 5 needles. Record your observations and draw and label your leaf in your journal.
  11. Obtain a moss plant. Look at the leaf-like and root-like structures with a hand lens. Record your observations and draw and label the plant in your journal.
  12. Obtain a microscope slide of a dicot leaf cross section (privet: Ligustrum). Examine the leaf under low power. Notice the arrangement of the various layers of the leaf. Identify the upper epidermis, the top layer of the leaf. Notice also the lower epidermis, the bottom layer of the leaf. Both epidermal layers help protect inner tissues (layers) of the leaf. You may see epidermal hairs, which cut down the intensity of bright light and slow down water evaporation. These hairs may make the leaf surface feel fuzzy.
  13. Focus the leaf cross section under high power and identify the structures inside the dicot leaf. Again locate the upper and lower epidermis. Note the size of the epidermal cells and the fact that the epidermis is a single layer of cells. Cells in the epidermis secrete a substance called cutin that covers the whole leaf. Cutin resists water and prevents the lea from drying out. Draw and label the cross section in your journal. Use colored pencils to show the structures as they appear on the slide.
  14. Move the slide so that you can focus on the lower epidermis. Examine closely the openings, or stomata, in the epidermis. Through the stomata, carbon dioxide passes into the leaf and oxygen and water vapor pass out of the leaf. Also locate the guard cells that border each stoma. These specialized cells change their shape to control the opening and closing of the stoma. Label the stoma on your drawing.
  15. Locate a layer of elongated, rectangular cells just below the upper epidermis. These palisade parenchyma cells contain many chloroplasts, which appear as tiny green bodies in the cells. When light strikes the chloroplasts, photosynthesis begins. Most photosynthesis occurs in the palisade cells. Label the palisade cells in your drawing.
  16. Just below the palisade cells, you will observe the irregularly shaped spongy parenchyma ells. Spongy cells contain fewer chloroplasts than palisade cells. The spongy cells form a honeycomb pattern with intercellular air spaces. These air spaces contain gases and connect with the stomata in the lower epidermis. Label the spongy layer on your drawing.
  17. Together the palisade cells and spongy cells make up the mesophyll layer. Label the mesophyll on your drawing.
  18. Move the slide sideways and locate a circular structure in the mesophyll layer. Each circular unit is a cross section of a leaf vein or vascular bundle. This view of a vein is similar to looking straight into a bundle of straws. Label the vascular bundle on your drawing.
  19. Find the xylem and phloem cells within the vascular bundles. Xylem cells generally have thick walls and are bunched towards the top of the vein. These cells carry water and minerals to the leaf. Phloem cells are thinner and are located towards the bottom of the vein. Phloem carries food from the leaf to the rest of the plant. Surrounding and supporting the vascular bundle are cells called the bundle sheaths. Label the xylem and phloem on your drawing.
  20. Find a microscope slide of a monocot (Zea-corn). Find the same features that you found on the dicot slide. Draw and label your drawing.

 

Lab Review Questions:

  1. Which surface, upper or lower, of the leaf contains the most chlorophyll, why?
  2. How is the structure that attaches the leaf to the stem different in monocots as compared to dicots?
  3. How are veins important to the leaf’s functions?
  4. How does the conifer leaf differ from the dicot leaf?
  5. How is a moss different from an angiosperm (monocot or dicot)?
  6. What are three major differences that can be used to tell a monocot leaf from a dicot leaf?
  7. What is the main function of the following cells found in the leaf?
    1. Upper epidermis
    2. Mesophyll
    3. Vascular bundle
    4. Stomata
  8. How do the stomata and air spaces work together to help the leaf survive?
  9. A cross section of a pine leaf would show a thickened cutin and a thickened epidermis. How does this design help the pine leaf to survive in northern United States?
  10. Some people mist their house plants with water. Will the plant be able to absorb water into its leaves? Why or why not?
