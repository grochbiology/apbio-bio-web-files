Because some of the Galápagos Islands reach heights of 1,000 meters, the climate at the higher elevations is very different than the climate near the shore. Thus on any one island, different “microhabitats ” exist because of the dramatically different temperatures and rainfall levels at different elevations. These habitats are referred to as “vegetative zones.”

The vegetative zones on the Galápagos Islands include a Coastal Zone along the ocean’s edge, an Arid Zone, a Scalesia Zone, a Miconia Zone, and a Fern Zone in the highlands. Each of these zones has a characteristic pattern of temperature and rainfall. As you venture from the coast and climb in altitude through the Arid Zone up to the Fern Zone, the amount of rainfall increases. The Fern Zone can receive as much as 2.5 meters of rainfall in a year. Of course, as the climates get wetter and cooler, the types of plants and animals that thrive are different from that of the drier areas. Each zone is a different community.

One way that scientists identify these ecological differences is with a climatogram, a graph that displays information about a climate. The precipitation is depicted as a bar graph with its scale on the left-hand side; the temperature is depicted as a line graph with its scale on the right-hand side. The patterns formed by these data for a given zone can be compared to patterns formed by the data for other zones.

In this activity, you will view a climatogram of the Coastal Zone of Santa Cruz Island and you will create a climatogram of the same island’s Fern Zone to compare the weather patterns of the two zones.
 
Procedure:
1.   Work in teams of three or four and print out a copy of the Student Printout Vegetative Zones of a Galápagos Island. Review this diagram and the descriptions of each zone. NOte the weather patterns and vegetation found in each zone in your journal.
 
2. The temperature and precipitation of each zone influences what organisms live there. To identify the weather patterns of a zone, scientists use a climatogram—a graph that clearly displays information about a climate. Examine the Climatogram of the Coastal Zone of Santa Cruz. What does the climatogram show about the zone’s annual precipitation and temperature?
 
3. Using the  Climate Data for the Fern Zone of Santa Cruz, create a climatogram of the Fern Zone using the data provided for the zone’s precipitation and temperature.


 

Lab Questions.

1.   Compare the climatogram of the Coastal Zone with the one you developed for the Fern Zone. How does the precipitation and temperature change from zone to zone? Which weather factor is more consistent? Which factor fluctuates throughout the year?
 
2. The Galápagos Islands usually have two seasons: a wet season and a dry season. Using the climatogram, determine when the wet season begins and how long it lasts.
 
3. Describe how plant and animal life might be affected by the wide fluctuations in rainfall in the Galápagos. Would you expect the Galápagos Islands to be home to plants and animals that are similar to those of the deserts of the American Southwest or the Everglades in Florida? Explain your answer.
 
4. At the summits of the higher islands, moisture from the clouds that hover over the peaks condenses onto the vegetation. How might this affect the growing conditions between the higher and lower elevations on these islands?
 
5. The prevailing winds cause the clouds (and the rain) to come from the south. How might this affect the size of the Arid Zone on the south side of an island? On the north side? How might this affect the Fern Zone on the south side of the island? On the north side?
