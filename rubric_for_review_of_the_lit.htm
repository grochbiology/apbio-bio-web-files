Rubric for Review of the Literature

Great overview of Review of the Literature: http://library.ucsc.edu/ref/howto/literaturereview.html

Part 1: General to specific.  (2 to 5 paragraphs)

    Includes a broad introduction to the subject you are investigating without mentioning your specific science fair project. The last sentence should reveal the thesis statement or question your review of the literature is evaluating. http://www.unc.edu/depts/wcweb/handouts/thesis.html 

    Includes cited references (using APA style)

    Includes a paragraph explaining how you did your search for related research. Example: Google was used to search for topics related to ____ using the following word search string: cattails  there were 891,000 hits. By adding "marsh" the number of hits was reduced to 299,000 and by finally adding sf bay  the hits were reduced to 51,000. Adding quotes around the search string reduced the number to 4 which was too limited. Google Scholar.....

    Last sentence should include the main organizing themes of the review the literature (which will also be the subject titles of the individual sections within the main body of the review of the literature. (Example. The effect of salt water intrusion on freshwater animals, the effect of global warming on salt water concentration, and the effect of saltwater on freshwater algae will be examined in this review...)

Part II: Organizing the body of the Review of the Literature: (5 to 16 paragraphs)

 http://www.unc.edu/depts/wcweb/handouts/literature_review.html  (see organizing the body)

 http://library.ucsc.edu/ref/howto/literaturereview.html  (see below for ideas that need to be addressed)

  • Place each work in the context of its contribution to the understanding of the subject under review
  • Describe the relationship of each work to the others under consideration
  • Identify new ways to interpret, and shed light on any gaps in, previous research

Part III: Sum up and do the following: ( 2 to 4 paragraphs)

  • Resolve conflicts amongst seemingly contradictory previous studies
  • Identify areas of prior scholarship to prevent duplication of effort
  • Point the way forward for further research
  • Place one's original work (in the case of theses or dissertations) in the context of existing literature
