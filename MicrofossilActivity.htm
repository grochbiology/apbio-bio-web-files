Microfossils Student Activity Kit (adapted from Flinn Scientific)

 

Introduction: Explore and classify actual microfossils that are over 375 years old

Concepts: Fossils, classification, biostratigraphy

 

Background:

            Microfossils are small, fossilized remains of organisms (animal, plant, or protozoa) that require magnification for study. They usually derive from the hard parts of organisms (skeletons, shells, jaws, teeth, seed coverings, etc). Microfossils may be imbedded in rock or exist as loose particles within soft substrate. The smallest microfossils may be only 0.001 mm (= 1 micron) and the largest up to 1-2 mm in size. Fossils larger than 2 mm are generally referred to as macrofossils.

            From the standpoint of research, microfossils are relied on heavily for research advances in fields such as biostratigraphy (the recognition of fossils and the relative position of their occurrences over space and time), paleoenvironmental reconstruction (reconstructing past environments), and paleooceanography (the study of oceans over time). Research studies of microfossils provide insights into ecological and evolutionary relationships among plant and animal communities that existed on Earth hundreds of millions of years ago.

            These microfossil specimens are from the Devonian Period (approximately 375 to 400 million years ago). The microfossils were isolated from soft deposits of shale and shale/limestone in Iowa, then sieved, washed, and dried to select for particle sizes between 0.3-1.0 mm.

 

Materials:

Stereoscope, microfossil spoon, rubber-tipped widget, Petri dish with lid (100 mm), Filter paper (125 mm), Water, Scissors

 

Procedure:

  1. Obtain a Petri dish; fill the bottom with water until the bottom is covered. (this is your sorting dish)
  2. Use the microfossil spoon to transfer a few spoonfuls of microfossil sievings into the sorting dish from the stock tube.
  3. Place the filter paper on the microscope stage, and use the light to shine on to the paper. Place the sorting dish on top of the filter paper… adjust for best illumination using 20x power.
  4. Obtain a rubber-tipped widget.
  5. Hold the widget as if it was a pencil and use the rubber tip to move the microfossils around in the Petri dish. Use the other hand to focus the microscope on the granules and gently move/swirl the dish to move fossils if necessary.
  6. Carefully scan the granules in the sorting dish, looking for shapes and textures characteristic of microfossils.
  7. Select a few to move to the dry lid of the Petri dish to be studied further.
  8. Carefully draw as much detail as possible of your fossils in your journal. (min. 5).
  9. Answer the classification questions for each fossil.
  10. Go online to Google images and see if you can identify your fossil.

 

Microfossil Questions:

  1. What are the key features (size, shape, markings, textures, etc) of each of the microfossils that provide key evidence as to the identity of each fossil?
  2. Describe the over all structure of the organisms.
  3. Is there any evidence to support whether or not the organisms lived as separate organisms or as a group (colony)?
  4. Do any of the organisms appear to be free-moving or sessile?
  5. Do any of these marine organisms still live today?
  6. What is the likely mineral or chemical composition of the microfossils?

 

Post-Lab Questions:

  1. Which categories of microfossils were most commonly found by the lab group? The class?
  2. Give possible reasons why microfossils in some categories may be more abundant than other microfossils.
  3. List the similarities and differences between the group/class microfossil specimens.
  4. Using the internet or a library answer the next two questions:
    1. Besides the categories of organisms shown in Table 1, what other marine organisms (invertebrate or vertebrate) were also abundant during the Devonian Period?
    2. Where were the continents located during the Devonian Period? Describe the global location/positions of the underwater masses were Devonian organisms must have been located (hint: Iowa).
