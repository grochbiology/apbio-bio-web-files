Dancing Dot Activity

From: Mickle & Aune (March 2011) “ A Simple, Inexpensive, Dynamic, & Hands-on Exercise for Prompting Discussion of the Characteristics of Living Things” The American Biology Teacher, Vol. 73, No. 3), pp. 164-166.

The Protocol

Equipment List

• one 100 mL beaker and one part of a Petri dish
• Isopropyl (rubbing) alcohol
• Forceps
• Tap water
• Hole-puncher and ordinary copier/printer paper or 15 to 20 dots hole-punched from ordinary copier/printer paper per experimental group
• Chemical splash goggles for each student
• Masking tape and pencil to label beakers
• Short movies of Euglena and/or Paramecium,
• 10 or 25 mL graduated cylinder
• A strainer for disposal (located in front lab bench sink)

Procedure

• Protective goggles must be worn for the duration of the exercise.
• Using the bottom half of a Petri dish,  add tap water to fill it about 3/4 to 7/8 full.
• Label the beaker “Rubbing alcohol” and add 10 mL of rubbing alcohol and 10 mL of tap water to make a 50% solution of rubbing alcohol.
• Put five paper dots in the rubbing-alcohol cup. Make sure that the paper is well soaked in the solution for 1–2 minutes.
• Using forceps take a dot out of the alcohol solution and gently place the flat side of the dot on the surface of the water in the other plastic cup.
Does it really matter if you place the dot on its edge instead of the flat side?
 • Observe, and repeat with the remaining dots. If a dot sinks, try another one. (record your observations in your journal).

Disposal

• Rubbing-alcohol solution and water can be poured through the strainer down the sink. Don’t let the paper dots go into the drain.
• Rinse the beakers and place back on the tray on your lab table and make sure the  paper dots are put in the trash.

 

Class Discussion Questions  (record your answers in your journal).

Short movies of Euglena and/or Paramecium,
• Are the Paramecium and Euglena that you saw in the video alive? Why or why not?
 
• Are the dancing dots alive? What characteristics might make you think that the dots are living things? Are these characteristics normally associated with living things?

• In what ways do living things differ from nonliving things?

Why does the dot move?

The paper dots move on the surface of the water because the mixing of the alcohol from the dot into the water weakens the surface tension of the water near the dot. This interaction creates an adjacent area of stronger surface tension, which pulls on the dot and causes it to move. Once the alcohol has dissipated, the surface tension regains equilibrium and movement ceases. This phenomenon is a completely physical phenomenon (Herbert and Ruchlis, 1968; Palewa, 2009).
