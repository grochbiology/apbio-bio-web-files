The Anti-Microbial Properties of Garlic

Objectives

1.      Use of semi-sterile techniques

2.      Measurement and timing of experiment steps

3.      Effects of a plant anti-microbial compound on a given bacteria

 

Pre-laboratory Questions

1.      Why would a plant make anti-microbial or insecticide compounds?

2.      Why is the agar surface covered with the bacteria before the disk is applied to the surface?

3.      Why is the extract passed through the column? 

 

Introduction

Plants produce a variety of insecticides and anti-microbial compounds as a means of defense.  In this laboratory, we are going to investigate the  anti-microbial compounds made by garlic.  Garlic has long been known as having anti-microbial properties.   Anti-microbial compounds can be what are referred to as high molecular weight or low molecular weight.   It is possible to separate the compounds based on this characteristic in the laboratory using column chromatography.  By passing an extract from garlic through a column that separates compounds base on molecular weight or size, we obtain fractions of the extract that can be tested for anti-microbial properties. 

The type of column chromatography used is referred to as gel filtration chromatography.  In essence, a column is prepared with a suspension of very small beads.  The beads have small channels running through the solid material they are prepared from providing added porosity to the gel.  As an extract is applied to the column, the larger molecules cannot fit into the small channels in the beads and therefore pass around the outside of the beads.  However, the smaller compounds can fit into the channels in the beads and therefore travel through the beads.  As a result, the larger compounds move much faster by traveling around the outside of the beads and leave the column through the outlet first.  The smaller compounds pass though the channels in the beads where they move slowly and exit the column later.  This allows the large and small compounds to be separated from each other. 

Once the compounds have been separated base on their size, they are tested for their ability to inhibit the growth of bacteria.   To begin, an agar plate must be covered with bacteria to provide a “lawn “ of bacteria that will grow in the absence of any inhibitory compounds.  To test the compounds extracted from garlic, a sterile disk is dipped into the each of these solutions and place on the bacteria surface. The plate is then incubated at a favorable temperature for the bacteria to grow.  After 24 hours the plate are removed and photographed to record the results.  If the compound that the disk was dipped in was able to inhibit growth of the bacteria, a clear area will be formed around the disk.  The strength of the compound can be evaluated based on the size of the clearing, thus a larger clearing indicates stronger inhibitory properties. 

 

 

Part I – Creating a Lawn of Bacteria

 

Materials

·          LB agar plate

·          E. coli culture

·          Sterile swabs

Procedure

1.      Dip the sterile swab into the E. coli culture and spread as demonstrated by the instructor.  Make sure the entire surface is covered.  Repeat if necessary.

2.      Allow the plate to stand at room temperature for 30 minutes. 

Part II – Preparation of Gel Filtration Column

Materials

·          De-ionized water

·          Pasteur pipette

·          Sephedex G25

·          Cotton

 

Procedure

1.      Obtain a Pasteur pipette and a small piece of cotton.

2.      Plug the narrow region of the pasture pipette using the cotton. 

3.      Holding the column over a beaker, run a few mL of de-ionized water through to wet the cotton. 

4.      Next, add the slurry of gel filtration material to the column while plugging the outlet with your finger.  Allow some of the gel material to begin accumulating in the area above the cotton before removing your finger. Once you remove your finger the water will begin flowing again.

5.      Add additional gel material until is it about 2-3 cm height in the pipette. 

6.      Continue adding water to the top of the pipette to keep the gel material moist at all times.  The column is now ready for the extract to be applied once it is prepared.

 

 

Part III - Extraction of Compounds from Garlic and Separation Based on Molecular Weight

Materials

·          Garlic clove

·          Balance

·          25 mL graduated cylinder

·          De-ionized water

·          Green onions

·          Mortal and pestles

·          Micro-centrifuge tubes

·          Centrifuge

 

 

Procedure

1.      Weight one clove of garlic and record the weight.  Try to use a clove that is between 3-5grams.

2.      Chop the clove into 3-5 smaller pieces and transfer them to the mortal. 

3.      Using the pestle, crush the garlic clove until it is lumpy but mushy.

4.      Add 1 volume of de-ionized water to the mortal with the garlic.  (If clove was 3 grams this means 3g*1volumes = 3ml of DI water)

5.      Continue to crush the garlic, add a 3-4 cm piece of green onion, and continue crushing.  

6.      When the garlic is fragrant and a light green color, the extraction is complete.

7.      Transfer the garlic slurry, avoiding large pieces into a micro centrifuge tube.

8.      Centrifuge at full speed for 5 minutes. 

9.      Pour the clear but green solution into a clean micro centrifuge tube.

10.  Check your column and allow the water to run through the column so that the water is 2-4 mm above the gel filtration material. 

11.  Apply the extract to the column by pipeting 0.5 ml of the clear green solution onto the column. 

12.  Place a clean micro centrifuge tube under the outlet of the column and collect the extract. 

13.  Allow half of the extracts to flow into the column then add water to fill the column above the gel filtration material. 

14.  Collect the extract until the green color gets very close to the bottom of the gel filtration material. This is when approximately 0.5 mL has been collected. 

15.  Now place another clean micro centrifuge tube under the outlet and continue adding water to the top of the column.

16.  Collect the extract until about 0.5 mL of solution has passed into the collection tube. 

 

 

Part IV – Transferring Extracted Compounds to the Lawn of Bacteria

Materials

·          LB agar plate prepared from Part I

·          Sterile disk

·          Tetracycline disk

·          Sterile tweezers

 

Procedure

1.      On the outside surface of the agar plate, designate where each disk will be placed.  It is important that the marking are discrete to allow observation through the agar after the incubation period. 

2.      Using the sterile tweezers grasp a single disk and dip it into the extract that was collected but before it was passed through the column.  (Solution from Part three  - Step 9)  Then place the disk in the designated area and gently press on the disk to ensure it will stay on the agar surface. 

3.      Again using the sterile tweezers grasp another disk and dip it into the large compound fraction that passed through the column. Then place the disk in the designated area and gently press on the disk to ensure it will stay on the agar surface. 

4.      Repeat step 3 but dip the disk into the small compound fraction that passed through the column.

5.       Obtain a tetracycline disk from instructor then place the disk in the designated area and gently press on the disk to ensure it will stay on the agar surface. 

6.      Incubate inverted at 37°C for 24 hours.  

7.      Observe the area around the disk.  If it is clear around a given disk, compared to the majority of the agar surface, this is positive result showing the compound inhibited the bacteria from growing.  This is referred to as a zone of inhibition. 

8.      Measure all of the zones of inhibition including tetracycline and record these results in your notebook. 

 

 

Discussion

At the conclusion of this laboratory, the students will be familiar with separation of compounds basis on size, testing a compound’s anti-microbial properties and how to interpret the results of a zone of inhibition.  The introduction of the report should describe the reasons why plants make these types of compounds and why humans are interested in isolating and identifying them.  The conclusion of the experiment should relate to the size of the zones of inhibition for the different extracts.  The zones of inhibition should be compared to the tetracycline and the relative effectiveness of the compounds discussed.  A suggestion to the students, to help them to draw conclusions, would be to have them rank the compounds based on size of zone of inhibition and this should lead them into a discussion and comparison between the compound extracts. 

 

 

 

Instructor Notes

The experiment takes one class session and about 15-20 minutes of the following session.  This laboratory can be overwhelming to the students with all the various steps.  It is helpful to demonstrate each part then have them perform that part and so forth for each section of the experimental procedure.   The green onion provides the green coloring to visualize the progress of the small molecules through the column.  The results can be very exciting to the students.
