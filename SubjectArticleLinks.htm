A Recipe for "Protolife"?

The earliest forms of "protolife" on Earth must have been able to reproduce and replicate, and it is likely that they were also cellular. But how did these primitive compartments come to be? In a report in the 31 Oct 2003 Science, Hanczyc et al. (http://www.sciencemag.org/cgi/content/short/302/5645/618) presented an experimental model of the primordial conditions that may have spawned life. They showed that clay particles can catalyze the formation of lipid vesicles in water. These same particles also adsorb RNA, and when forced to divide (by filtration through small pores), distribute the RNA into daughter vesicles. The chemical energy needed to drive vesicle formation in this system derived from a decrease in pH and the subsequent phase change in the lipids from micelles to bilayer membrane. The experiments thus demonstrate that vesicle growth and division can result from simple physicochemical forces, without any complex biochemical machinery. Similar conditions may have existed in porous mounds of clay and other minerals on the ancient ocean floor, where alkaline seepage from hydrothermal sources could have provided the necessary inputs of material and energy for life to take form. Although there is no evidence that natural lipids would have been present in sufficient quantities, amino acids could have easily joined together as polypeptides to form the first organic membranes. A Perspective by M. J. Russell (http://www.sciencemag.org/cgi/content/short/302/5645/580) highlighted the report.

Ozone Depletion and Antarctic Climate

Much attention has been paid to the influence of increased greenhouse gas concentrations on global climate, but human-induced ozone depletion has also been implicated in affecting climate change. In a report in Science last year (3 May 2002, p. 895), Thompson and Solomon argued that seasonal stratospheric ozone depletion over Antarctica was affecting weather in the lower atmosphere (troposphere) and was at least partly responsible for the observed climate warming and strengthening of westerly winds encircling the southern polar cap. A report by Gillett and Thompson (http://www.sciencemag.org/cgi/content/short/302/5643/273) in the 10 Oct 2003 Science has now provided strong evidence to support that hypothesis. The researchers presented modeling data on the seasonality, spatial structure, and amplitude of high-latitude climate change in the Southern Hemisphere and showed that the observed changes in spring and summer can indeed be explained as a response to human-induced Antarctic ozone depletion. As noted in an accompanying Perspective by D. J. Karoly (http://www.sciencemag.org/cgi/content/short/302/5643/236), it is the first modeling study to show quantitative agreement between climate variability at Earth's surface and ozone depletion in the stratosphere. Still, recent climate change in the Southern Hemisphere has likely resulted from a combination of natural and human influences, and untangling the separate contributions will be a crucial next step toward predicting how climate may change in the future.

Battling Brain Disease

http://www.sciencemag.org/cgi/content/summary/302/5646/807
 

In recent years, our understanding of the basic mechanisms underlying brain diseases has grown tremendously. The 31 Oct 2003 Science explored some of the field's most exciting developments -- and the prospects for future prevention and therapy. Review and Viewpoint articles examined the state of our knowledge of Parkinson's disease, the genetic complexities underlying many neurodegenerative and neuropsychiatric disorders (and the promise of new techniques for early detection and treatment), the havoc caused by misfolded proteins in Alzheimer's disease and prion disorders, and updates to our understanding of two postnatal developmental disorders in young children, autism and Rett syndrome. News stories focused on efforts by psychiatrists to overhaul their current scheme for diagnosing mental illness to incorporate input from the fields of genetics, neuroscience, and behavioral science; and on hopeful new strategies for treating depression, despite researchers' still incomplete understanding of the disease's biology. In addition, research papers in the same issue provided new insight into the molecular underpinnings of prion disease, Rett syndrome, and Parkinson's disease.

Online, articles at Science's STKE (http://stke.sciencemag.org/misc/scifeatured/SciRelatedArticles.dtl) discussed the contributions of neurotransmitter transporters in psychiatric disorders, the possible role of the phosphatase calcineurin in schizophrenia, and evidence for the signaling functions of huntingtin -- a protein altered in Huntington's disease. A case study detailed on Science's SAGE KE (http://sageke.sciencemag.org/) offered further insight into Huntington's disease with pathology slides and a movie of a patient's neurological testing. And a special Science Online Web supplement (http://www.sciencemag.org/sciext/braindisease/) offered links to additional resources and articles on the diseases explored in the special issue.

Researchers Define Molecular Basis of Human "Sweet Tooth" and Umami Taste

Halloween turns millions of kids into candy-loving monsters with more than ample supply of confections to satisfy their "sweet tooth." Now, HHMI researchers have moved closer to understanding why some people cannot resist the impulses brought on by sweets.

The researchers created mice with the same sweet-tooth preferences as humans by inserting the gene that codes for a human sweet-taste receptor protein into the animals. They also inserted an entirely different receptor gene into the taste cells of mice, thereby producing animals that perceive a previously tasteless molecule as sweet.For the full story, go to http://www.hhmi.org/news/zuker5.html
