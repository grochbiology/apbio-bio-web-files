Population Genetics and Evolution:  A Simulation

Overview

   In this activity you will learn about the Hardy-Weinberg principle of genetic equilibrium and study the relationship between evolution and changes in allele frequency by using your class as a sample population.

Objectives

   At the completion of this activity you should be able to:

  1. Calculate allele and genotype frequencies using the Hardy-Weinberg theorem.
  1. Discuss the effect of natural selection on allelic frequencies.
  2. Explain and predict the effect on allelic frequencies of selection against the homozygous  

             recessive.

  1. Discuss the relationship between evolution and changes in allele frequencies as measured by       deviations from the Hardy-Weinberg principle of genetic equilibrium

Background

   In 1908, G.H. Hardy and W. Weinberg independently suggested a scheme whereby evolution could be viewed as changes in the frequency of alleles in a population of organisms.  They reasoned that if A and a are alleles for a particular gene locus and each diploid individuals has two such loci, then p could be designated as the frequency of the A allele and q as the frequency of the a allele.  Thus, in a population of 100 individuals (each with two copies of the gene) in which 40% of the genes are A, p would be 0.40.  The rest of the genes (60%) would be a, and q would be equal to 0.60 (i.e. p + q = 1.0).  These are referred to as allele frequencies.  If certain conditions are met, the frequency of the possible diploid combinations of these alleles (AA, Aa, aa) should equal p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1.0.  Hardy and Weinberg also argued that if five conditions are met, the population's allele and genotype frequencies will remain constant from generation to generation.  These conditions are:

1. The population is very large.  The effects of chance on changes in allele frequencies is thereby     greatly reduced.

2. Individuals show no mating preference for A or a (mating is random).

3. There are no new mutations of the alleles for the gene.

4. There is no immigration or emigration of individuals and their genes in or out of the    

     population.

5. All genotypes have an equal chance of surviving and reproducing (there is no selection).

Basically, the Hardy-Weinberg equation describes the status quo. If the five conditions are met, then no change will occur in either allele or genotype frequencies in the population.  Of what value is such a rule?  It provides a yardstick by which changes in allele frequency, and therefore evolution, can be measured.  One can look at a population and ask: Is evolution occurring with

respect to a particular gene locus?  The Hardy-Weinberg principle allows for the detection of changes in the population's gene frequency over time (evolution).

Design of the Experiment

   In order for the experiment to work, the five Hardy-Weinberg assumptions must be met.  In most instances, the class population at one school site will not be large enough to meet the large population size assumption.  Therefore, pooled data from several sites will be combined for analysis.

Hardy-Weinberg Population - No Selection

   It will be assumed that the class is initially a population of randomly mating heterozygous individuals, each of genotype Aa.  Therefore, the initial allele frequency is 0.5 for the dominant allele A and for the recessive allele a, that is, p = 0.5 and q = 0.5.

   Each person will receive four index cards.  Two cards will have the letter A printed on them and two cards will have the letter a.  A represents the dominant allele and a is a recessive allele.  The four cards represent the products of meiosis from the genotype Aa.

Procedure

  1. Find a partner at random.  The sex of the partner does not matter (mate randomly).  Turn the four cards over so the letters are not showing.  Shuffle the cards and take the card on top to contribute to the production of the first offspring.  Your partner should do the same.  Put the two cards together.  You are now the proud parents of the first offspring!  One person should record the genotype of this offspring in the appropriate generation row of Table 2.  This is the genotype the first person will assume in the next generation.

  1. Reshuffle the cards and repeat the mating event.  The second person should record the genotype of this offspring in their Table 2. This is the genotype this person will assume in the  next generation.

  1. Each student should remark their cards, if necessary, to represent the possible alleles of their new genotype.  For example, if the new genotype is AA, the only alleles this person can give is the dominant A.  All four cards must be changed to A!  If the new genotype is aa, the only alleles would be a and all four cards must be changed to a.  If the genotype is Aa, two cards should be A and two cards should be a (what you started out with).

  1. Continue mating at random until you have data for 8 generations.  Be sure to locate someone of the same generation  before mating.  This will help prevent interbreeding between the generations.
