French Fries, Diffusion, & Osmosis

(Adapted from Meis Friedrichsen, P., & Pallant, A. (2007). French Fries, Dialysis Tubing & Computer Teaching Difffusin & Osmosis Through Inquiry & Modeling. The American Biology Teacher, February2007 (Osmosis & Diffusion), 22-27.)

Part 1:

Question: What happens to raw French fries when soaked in water?

Demo:

3- 500 mL Beakers, raw potatoes- sliced without skins, distilled water, 0.9% & 10% NaCl

Procedure:

Day 1:

1.      Slice raw potato into 3 fries, obtain initial mass (g) of each potato.

2.      Put one fry in each beaker, beaker A (distilled water), beaker B (0.9% NaCl), beaker C (10% NaCl).

3.      Work with your lab group, draw a picture of each beaker and discuss and draw what you predict (hypothesize) may be happening in each of the beakers in your own journal. 

4.      Share out of predictions to the class.

5.      Allow beakers to sit out overnight covered with plastic wrap.

Day 2:

1.      Observe the fries. How do the fries appear, describe the condition of the fries in your journal.

2.      Measure the final mass of each potato.

3.      Answer the following question in your group: What do you think is happening at the molecular and cellular level in each of the potatoes (from each beaker)?

4.      Share out ideas to the class.

Question: Which of these substances will pass through dialysis tubing: glucose, starch or water?

1.      In your lab group, make a prediction (hypothesis).

• What did you observe during exploration? • What is puzzling about your observations? • What questions are raised? • What is the central causal question? • What are some possible explanations (hypotheses)? • How might these explanations be tested? • What are the expected/predicted results of each explanation and planned test?

2.      Design a way to test your prediction using 2% Iodine (Lugol’s solution) 10% glucose, 10% starch, & distilled water. You can use any of the following items located on the front bench. (12 cm length dialysis tubing soaking in water, beakers, test tubes & test tube racks, funnels, microscope slides, plastic pipettes, string, tape, rubber bands, glucose test strips (get from teacher), and electronic balances.)

3.      Design necessary data tables to collect your evidence/support for your prediction.

4.      Technical advice: Remember that Lugol’s solution can detect the presence of starch. Dialysis tubing can be knotted at both ends to create an artificial cell or can be cut along its length creating a larger, flat piece of membrane.

5.      The teacher is only available to provide equipment,

6.      You can set up your experiment to run overnight. (Not required). You can come in at lunch or after school to redo, retry, redesign if necessary.

QQuestions to consider while running your experiments:

• Following your test, what are your observed results?
• How do your observed and expected results compare?
• If observed and expected results do not match, is the mismatch attributable to a faulty hypothesis, a faulty test, a faulty deduction, or some combination? Can you tell? Why or why not?
• If observed and expected results match, what conclusion should you draw? Have your results eliminated the alternatives? If not, what additional tests are needed?
• Can you be sure that the match or mismatch of observed and expected results is not attributable to chance? If not, what can you do to reduce the likelihood of drawing an incorrect conclusion?

Day 3:

1.      In your lab group record your results (if your experiment ran overnight).

2.      As a group, formulate (conclude) about the permeability (porousness) of the dialysis tubing to the various solutions (dH2O, glucose, & starch solutions). Are your predictions supported by your evidence?

3.      Pick a spokesperson to share out your experimental design and results, share any challenges.

4.      You are encouraged to ask questions about other table’s findings.  If your group’s findings are discrepant (disagreeing with the other groups’ findings). Please either redesign or retest at lunch or after school.

1.      What happens to raw French fries when they are soaked in water?

2.      What happens to raw French fries when soaked in 0.9% NaCl and 10% NaCl? (Which solution would result in crisper fries?)
