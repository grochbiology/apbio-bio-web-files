General Paper Requirements/ Structure/ Transition Words

 

 

Format:     The paper will be in standard Modern Language Association (MLA) format.  The paper must be typed, double spaced with one-inch margins and no larger than 12-point font.  Do not use bold or italicized text.  Do not use a cover page.  Write your name, class period and course title in the upper right corner with a centered title on the next line.  Put page numbers in the top right header of each page.

 

Length:    Two pages not including bibliography page.

 

Sources:  There must be a minimum of THREE sources.  Acceptable sources include periodicals, newspapers, encyclopedia, books, interviews, and the Internet.  Only use ONE encyclopedia or class textbook source and no more than TWO Internet sources.  All Internet sources must be credible. 

 

Citation:  All information copied or paraphrased must be cited correctly.  To cite information correctly, put the author’s last name and the page number in parenthesis directly after the sentence or short passage you are quoting or paraphrasing.  Use quotation marks with any information that is three or more words of another author.  Cite all paraphrased information that is not common knowledge.  Common knowledge means that it can be found in five or more sources.   Failure to cite sources and not to include a bibliography on this paper will result in zero points on this assignment!

 

Structure:     The paper must include an introduction with a topic that incorporates three main ideas. The paper should also include a minimum of three supporting body paragraphs that include a topic sentence, concrete details and commentary. The paper should close with a concluding paragraph.  (More details on structure below)

 

Bibliography: All sources must be listed correctly, in alphabetical order on a separate Bibliography page at the end of the paper. (Refer to the “Bibliography” handout for more information.)

 

Do's and Don'ts:

·     The thesis paper is formal writing and must be written in the third person.

·     Do not use the first person or personal pronouns (I, we, us, our, your, you). Unless directed to by your instructor.

·     Do not use contractions, abbreviations or slang

·     Write out numbers at the beginning of a sentence.

·     All verbs should be in the same tense.  Use the past or present tense but do not switch.

·     Do not use vague pronouns such as “they”, ‘their”, "it" or “many”.

 

NO LATE PAPERS WILL BE ACCEPTED!

 

 

Paper Structure

Introduction (1 paragraph)

The introduction should grab the reader's attention; give background information on your topic and end with the thesis statement. The introduction should be about one page.

Opener:                                     

Open up with a quote, interesting fact or a brief story.

Background Information:    

Define concepts; describe countries, people, places, and history associated with the topic. Pretend the reader knows nothing. Use your note cards and cite sources for background information.  Put the author’s last name and the page number of the book in parenthesis at the end of the sentence. (Author Pg.#).

Transitions:    (see below)         

Use transitions throughout the paragraph to connect your sentences.

Thesis Statement: 

End the paragraph with your final thesis statement.

 

Body Paragraphs (3 to 4 paragraphs)

Each body paragraph should be an argument that supports or proves your thesis.  Write one paragraph for each argument. Your arguments should have concrete details and commentary that connects  to the thesis.  The paragraph should flow smoothly connected by transitions. Each body paragraph should be about one page long.

 Topic Sentence:    

Introduce your argument in the topic sentence.

 Concrete Detail #1:    

State the first example that supports or proves the argument.

Quote:                      

Use a quote from research.  Be sure to CITE THE SOURCE.  Put the author’s last name and the page number of the book in parenthesis at the end of the sentence. (Author #).

Commentary:         

Explain the concrete detail and the quote.  Then explain how the example proves the argument and the thesis.

Transitions:   (see below)          

Use transitions throughout the paragraph to connect your sentences.

(The first example..., The second example..., Another example...)

 Conclusion Sentence:            

Restate your argument in the last sentence of the paragraph.

  

Conclusion (1 paragraph)

The conclusion should summarize your arguments and leave the reader feeling convinced you have proven your thesis.  A good conclusion shows you are organized and convincing. The conclusion should be one-half a page in length.

Topic Sentence:               

 Transition from your arguments

Restate Arguments:         

Clearly restate your arguments

Conclude:                           

Finish with strong concluding commentary and final thoughts

Option:                               

 You can start or end with a quote.  I prefer to end in my own words.

 

 

Transitions

 

Transitions are the glue that holds a paper together. Without them the paper is a list of random ideas that have no connection. Please look at the list of words below. They will help your ideas and sentences together.

 

 

 

1.     ADDITION: again, and, and then, also, besides, further, furthermore, in addition, moreover, equally important, likewise, next, first, second, third, in the first place, in the second place, finally, last.

2.     ADDITION and SERIES: to begin with, first of all, first; second, secondly, next; third, thirdly; to conclude, finally.

3.    COMPARISON: similarly, likewise, in like manner, by comparison, in comparison to, compared with.

4.    CONTRAST: but, yet, and yet, however, still, nevertheless, on the other hand, on the contrary, by contrast, after all, differing from, notwithstanding, for all that, in contrast, at the same time, although, even though, otherwise.

5.     PLACE: away from, beyond, here, nearby, opposite to, close to, adjacent to, on the opposite side, next to, in close proximity.

6.     PURPOSE: to this end, for this purpose, with this object in mind, for this reason.

7.     RESULT: hence, therefore, consequently, accordingly, thus, thereupon, as a result, then.

8.   SUMMARY, REPETITION, EXEMPLICATION, INTENSIFICATION: to sum up, to summarize, in brief, on the whole, in short, as I have stated, in other words, that is, that is to say, to be sure, as has been noted, for example, for instance, in fact, indeed, to tell the truth, in any event.

9.    TIME: meanwhile, at length, immediately, soon after, soon, after, afterward, in the meantime, later, finally.

Rubric Guidelines:

1. Credible thesis statement (topic sentence)
2. Background information complete; all scientific jargon explained.
3. Pro argument; at least two credible arguments with evidence.
4. Con argument; at least two credible arguments with evidence.
5. Any quotes clearly attributed to sources listed in references.
6. References; use correct format and at least one print source.
