Evolution ECA w/ learning objectives

BIG IDEA 1: The process of evolution drives the diversity and unity of life.

1. Natural selection is a major mechanism of evolution.

Learning Objective:

a. able to convert a data set from a table of numbers reflecting a change in the genetic makeup of a population over time

b. apply mathematical methods to investigate the cause(s) and effect(s) of this change.

c. can evaluate evidence provided by data /sets/ to qualitatively and quantitatively investigate the role of natural selection in evolution.

d. able to use data from a real or simulated population, based on graphs or models of types of selection

e. apply mathematical methods to predict what will happen to the population in the future.

Assessment#1: Go over Self Quiz the chapter 23 on p. 486. Complete question 7 (Scientific inquiry).

2. Natural selection acts on phenotypic variations in populations.

an illustrative example such as: Sickle cell, Yellow mice (agouti)

Learning Objective:

a. able to evaluate data-based evidence that describe evolutionary changes in the genetic makeup of a population over time.

b. can connect evolutionary changes in a population over time to a change in the genetic variation or make-up of the population.

Assessment #2: Explain how sickle cell anemia affects the variation of phenotype within the human population (heterozygote advantage) and/or the agouti mouse (as described on the Ghost in your Genes)

3. Evolutionary change is also driven by genetic drift and artificial selection.

Learning Objective:

a. uses data from mathematical computer models based on the HW-equilibrium to analyze genetic drift and artificial selection in the evolution of specific populations.

b. able to make predictions about the effects of genetic drift on the genetic makeup of a population.

Assessment #3: Explain how bottleneck events can lead to shifts in allele frequencies. Name a historical event that lead to genetic drift.

4. Biological evolution is supported by evidence from many scientific disciplines.

Learning Objective:

a. able to evaluate evidence provided by data sets from many scientific disciplines to support biological evolution.

b. able to design a plan to collect data from morphology, biochemistry and geology to answer scientific questions regarding how organisms have changed over time.

c. can connect phenomena across spatial and temporal scales to articulate the claim that biological evidence of evolution is supported by evidence from many scientific disciplines.

Assessment #4: you found a jawbone on a trip to the Olduvai gorge in Africa. Explain how you would know the approximate age based on morphology and geology. If the jawbone dated to less than 25,000 years old would there be anyway to check for DNA homology?

5. A phylogenetic tree and/or a cladogram is a graphical representation (model) of evolutionary history that can be tested.

Learning Objective:

a. able to pose scientific questions that identify the essential shared characteristics (derived and primitive) of a group (or groups) of organisms whose relatedness is described by a phylogenetic tree in order to infer the evolutionary history of the group and to identify character data that potentially could extend/improves the phylogenetic tree hypothesis.

b. able to evaluate evidence provided by a data set in a phylogenetic tree to determine evolutionary history and speciation.

Assessment #5: Extend the Bears and Panda activity to include a bacteria (ex. E.coli), a fungi (ex. penicillium), a protist (ex. Euglena), corn (Zea mays) whichever organisms are available at NCBI, using a phylogenetic tree, which organism is the most closely related to the bears (how do you know)?

6. Speciation may occur when two populations become reproductively isolated from each other.

Learning Objective:

a. is able to justify the selection of a data set from a group of data sets that answers the question ―Does reproductive isolation lead to speciation?‖

b. is able to describe the speciation in an isolated population and connect it to change in gene frequency, change in environment, natural selection, and genetic drift.

Assessment #6: using Darwin's finches, describe the process of allopatric and sympatric speciation using the concept of genetic drift, pre and post zygotic barrier to reproductive isolation.

7. Populations of organisms continue to evolve. using an illustrative example such as:

Chemical resistance (in that mutations for resistance to antibiotics occur in bacteria regardless of the presence of antibiotics; pesticides, herbicides; cancer chemotherapy)

Emergent diseases

Learning Objective: The student is able to describe a population that has evolved.

Assessment #7: using an illustrative example such as: Chemical resistance (in that mutations for resistance to antibiotics occur in bacteria regardless of the presence of antibiotics; OR pesticides, OR herbicides; OR cancer chemotherapy OR HIV treatments to describe micro evolution). How are emergent diseases (H1N1, bird flu, etc.) also under pressure to evolve?

8. There are causal models about the origin of life on Earth.

Learning Objective:

a. is able to describe scientific models about the origin of life on Earth.

b. is able to evaluate questions about the scientific theories about the origin of life on earth.

c. is able to describe the reasons for revisions of scientific theories of the origin of life on earth.

d. is able to evaluate scientific models about the origin of life on earth.

Assessment #8: Address Learning objectives a-d.

9. Evidence from many different scientific disciplines supports models of the origin of life.

Learning Objective:

a. is able to justify geologic, physical, and chemical data that supports model construction of early Earth conditions and describe experiments that produce monomers and polymers of organic molecules.

Assessment #9: Discuss Miller and Urey's experiment, what changes have been added to their original hypothesis? Does panspermia have support? Discuss abiogenesis as it relates to the origin hypotheses presented in your book.
