Chromosome Flip Book (by J. Nevis)

 

You will be making a chromosome flip book to represent crossing over during meiosis.  During meiosis, homologous chromosomes line up next to each other forming a tetrad (four sister chromatids).  By lining up next to each other, the non-sister chromatids reshuffle genes with each other (for this activity, we will assume that the genes are reshuffling independently of each other, that they are not linked together…but that is another story that will be dealt with later J). 

 

Directions:  Work in partners, each partner will be in charge of one chromosome and each person will have their own flipbook. 

 

  1. Make a book for your chromosomes by taking two sheets of blank paper, folding in half (like a hamburger) and stapling together at the folded edge.  See teacher example.
  2. On the cover, write “Crossing over homologous chromosomes of….”  Pick something that you would like to cross.  Can be real or imagined, animate or inanimate; for example: a dog, purses, phones, happy faces, cars. 
  3. On the second page, draw a large X to represent one of the homologous chromosomes.  Make sure the lines go from corner to corner.
  4. Using a different colored marker, make another X in the same way on the next (third) page
  5. On the last page, write “Four possible offspring” at the top.
  6. Pick five traits about your object that interest you. 
  7. For each of the five traits, choose two forms of that trait.  For example, suppose one trait is eyebrows;  decide what about that trait you want to have on your chromosome; for example: long or short, wide or narrow, bushy or thin, uni- or two.  After that, decide which forms of each trait are dominant and which are recessive. 
  8. Now, write the traits onto the tetrad chromosomes; observe the teacher example.  Three of the five traits on the homologous chromosomes must be heterozygous and two must be homozygous.
  9. Cut between the traits on the first chromosome only.  You now have a flipbook representing five traits on homologous chromosomes during meiosis.
  10. Flip the book and write out all of the 10 possible gamete trait combinations (see teacher example).  Write these in the tables below for each person.  Cut them out and keep separate from your partner’s.  These are the possible gametes that can be passed on to the offspring of your organism.

  1. Let’s have some “Self fertilization Fun!”:  Randomly pair up one of your gametes with one of your partner’s gametes.  On the forth page in your flipbook, write out the genotypes of the gametes (person 1 gamete X person 2 gamete).
  2. Write out the genotype for the zygote (offspring).
  3. Draw the offspring’s phenotype using the genotype from the cross of you and your partner’s gametes.
  4. Write the phenotype of the traits under your offspring drawing.  Color your drawing.  Use the back for extra space.
  5.  **Each person in a pair will do this so you will not necessarily have the same offspring as your partner.
  6. Repeat steps 11-14 three more times.

 

Grading:           3 points for chromosomes (genes labeled dominant/recessive)
