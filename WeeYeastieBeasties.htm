Wee Yeastie Beasties Inquiry Activities                        

 

Introduction:  Yeast is the common name given to unicellular (single celled) ascomycetes fungi.  They are characterized as being heterotrophic eukaryotes. The yeast you'll be using is baker's yeast (Genus species, Saccharomyces cerevisiae). This yeast reproduces asexually by fission (splitting) or by budding off a larger cell.  Sexual reproduction occurs when two cells types (a and alpha) fuse together.  Yeast is a choice organism for scientists to use in molecular and cellular research.  We're interested in investigating the process of cellular respiration.  (C6H12O6 + 6O2 -> 6CO2 + 6H2O, the oxidation of glucose).

 

DAY 1

Demonstration #1: 

Materials: 12mL syringe, yeast solution, sugar source, large beaker, aquarium tubing, bromthymol blue solution, water, 10 mL graduated cylinder.

 

Demonstration #2:

Materials: 15mL graduated centrifuge tube (with hole in cap), yeast solution, sugar source, beaker, water.

 

Post Demonstration Questions:

1.        What was the purpose of using a sugar solution?

2.       What was the purpose of the bromthymol blue?

3.       Write the equation for oxidation of glucose, labeling the reactants and the products?

4.       Why were yeast used in these demonstrations? 

5.       Compare and contrast the results of both demonstrations?

 

 

Class Activity:

In your lab group, ask a question about the cellular respiration of yeast.

Here are Some Sample Inquiry Questions:

What else can yeast use as an energy source? 

Does temperature play any part in cellular respiration?

 

Lab Directions:

Each table will be assigned an experiment to test the cellular respiration rate of yeast using a 5% solution of various sugars (lactose, maltose, fructose, glucose, sucrose, and water as a control). Now write out your hypothesis, null hypothesis, IV, DV, SV and control setup.

 

For each group of 2 students:

1. Measure out 0.2 grams of yeast and place in the bottom of a 15 ml tube (the cap has a small pin hole).

2. Add about 7 ml of the assigned solutions, place the cap on the tube and your finger over the hole, mix until the yeast appears dissolved.

3. Add more of the assigned solution to the 14 ml mark on the tube, cap, and remix.

4. Fill the Styrofoam cup with warm water (~35-40ºC) use a thermometer and record the temperature as you run the experiment.

5. With your finger over the hole, flip the capped tube over and submerge in the warm water, note there should be about 1 ml of air at the top of the tube, if there is any yeast stuck at the top remix and then re-submerge in the warm water.

6. Record the amount of air space at the top of the tube each minute for at least 35 minutes or until the tube empties into the water.  (Be patient, those yeast need time to wake up and start "respiring".)

7. Clean up your cup and tubes with soapy water and make sure there is no residue yeasty smell.
